When and how to appeal a grade:

You can appeal a grade if you believe there was a calculation error, if the grading was inconsistent with the syllabus, or if there was bias or misconduct in the evaluation. You cannot appeal just because you disagree with the professor's assessment or wish the grade were higher.

Step 1: Talk to the professor first. Email them and ask to meet. Come with specific evidence — your exam, the rubric, specific questions you believe were graded incorrectly. Be calm and factual. Many grade disputes are resolved at this stage.

Step 2: If that fails, go to the department chair. Most departments have a formal grade appeal process. Ask the department administrative coordinator for the form or process. You'll need to submit written documentation of your complaint and any evidence.

Step 3: The dean's office or academic standards committee handles escalated appeals. These take longer, sometimes a full semester, and have a higher bar for overturning grades — you need documented evidence of error or bias, not just dissatisfaction.

Document everything. Save all emails, graded assignments, syllabi, and written feedback. If you talk to someone in person, follow up with an email summarizing the conversation.

Reply: The formal grade appeal process exists and professors know it. In my experience, many professors will reconsider when they see you know the process. Not because they fear it — because it signals you're serious.
