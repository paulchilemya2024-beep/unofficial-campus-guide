First-generation college student tips from people who've been there:

You don't know what you don't know — and that's normal. Most college knowledge is passed down from parents who went to college. If your parents didn't go, you're figuring it out without that cheat code. That's harder but not impossible.

Ask for help early and often. Professors, advisors, upperclassmen, office staff — they are there to help you. Many of them went out of their way to get where they are specifically because they want to help. The worst anyone will say is I don't know, try this other office.

Find the first-gen support office or program at your school. Most universities now have specific programs — they have advisors who specialize in helping first-gen students navigate financial aid, registration, career planning, and grad school. These are hugely underused.

Imposter syndrome is real and almost universal. The feeling that you don't belong there, that everyone else knows more, that you're going to be found out — this is experienced by first-gen students and high-achieving students alike. You do belong there. You got in for a reason.

Go to office hours. First-gen students often avoid office hours because they feel like it's showing weakness. The opposite is true. Students who go to office hours get better grades, better recommendations, and better research opportunities. Professors remember names. Names get opportunities.

Reply: I graduated, got into a PhD program, and still feel like an imposter sometimes. The feeling doesn't go away — but it does stop running your decisions if you learn to recognize it.
