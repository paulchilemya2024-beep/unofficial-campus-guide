Balancing a part-time job with college — what actually works:

Cap your work hours at 15-20 per week during the semester. Research consistently shows academic performance drops significantly above 20 hours. If you're working more than 20 hours, have a real conversation with yourself about whether you need to adjust your course load, look for higher-paying work, or find emergency financial resources.

Work-study jobs are underrated. If your aid package includes work-study, prioritize it. Work-study jobs are designed to flex around your class schedule. Supervisors are usually campus staff who understand finals week. Many work-study positions like library desk or research assistant also let you study during slow periods.

On-campus jobs in general are easier than off-campus — no commute, schedule flexibility, and sometimes perks like gym access or dining credits. Check your school's student employment office.

Use a time block schedule, not a to-do list. Assign specific hours to class, studying, work, and rest. Treat study time like a job shift — you show up whether you feel like it or not.

Take advantage of campus resources to reduce financial pressure: food pantries, emergency grants, textbook lending, free campus events with food. Reducing other expenses means you can work fewer hours.

Reply: I worked 18 hours a week all four years and graduated with a 3.4. The key was never scheduling work the night before an exam and being honest with my supervisor when things got intense. They worked with me every time.
