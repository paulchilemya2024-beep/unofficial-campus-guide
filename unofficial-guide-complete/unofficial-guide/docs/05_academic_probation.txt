Academic probation feels terrifying but it's survivable. Here's what you actually need to do:

Read the exact terms of your probation letter. Schools differ — some require you to hit a 2.0 GPA next semester, some require cumulative GPA improvement. Know exactly what the target is so you can plan.

Meet with your academic advisor immediately. Don't avoid them out of embarrassment. Advisors have seen this many times. They can help you figure out which courses to retake (grade replacement policies vary — some schools replace the old grade, some average both), and which courses to take for maximum GPA recovery.

Reduce your course load if you can. Taking 12 units and getting a 3.5 is better than taking 18 units and getting a 2.0. Academic probation is a sign your load needs adjusting, not a punishment for being dumb.

Use the tutoring center aggressively. It's free. Most students don't use it. Students who do use it consistently pass at much higher rates. Make an appointment for every subject you're struggling in.

Talk to professors during office hours especially early in the semester. If a professor knows your name and sees your effort, they will often work with you on makeup work, exam retakes, and extensions in ways they wouldn't for anonymous students.

Reply: I went from a 1.8 to a 3.1 over two semesters by doing exactly this. Retook two courses with grade replacement, used tutoring for everything, dropped from 18 to 14 units. It's possible.
