Student loan basics most students don't learn until they're already in debt:

Know the difference between subsidized and unsubsidized loans. Subsidized loans — the government pays the interest while you're in school. Unsubsidized — interest starts accruing immediately, even while you're enrolled. Always take subsidized loans first, unsubsidized second, and private loans never if you can avoid it.

Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) plans: If you graduate and can't afford the standard payment, you can switch to an IDR plan where your payment is capped at a percentage of your discretionary income (as low as 5% under the SAVE plan). After 20-25 years of payments, the remaining balance is forgiven. Apply at studentaid.gov.

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF): If you work for a government or nonprofit employer and make 120 qualifying payments on an IDR plan, the remaining balance is forgiven tax-free. Huge benefit if you're going into public service, education, healthcare, or social work.

If you're struggling to make payments, call your loan servicer and ask about deferment or forbearance options. These pause your payments temporarily. Not ideal since interest may still accrue, but better than missing payments and damaging your credit.

Never miss a federal loan payment without contacting your servicer first. Default has severe consequences — wage garnishment, tax refund seizure, credit destruction. There are always options before default.

Reply: Use the loan simulator at studentaid.gov to model different repayment plans with your actual balance. It takes 10 minutes and shows you clearly which plan costs the least over time.
